### 2\. Sanity: Best for structured content and automation

![Image of Sanity homepage](/portfolio/prismic/060-best-headless-cms-for-developers/image-5.webp)

[Sanity](https://www.sanity.io/) positions itself as a content operating system that provides teams with a solid foundation for handling a wide range of content workflows. At the heart of Sanity are three main layers:

- **Content Lake** stores content as structured data so it can be reused and adapted across any front end.
- **Compute and AI** handles automation, custom logic, and smarter workflows instead of treating content as static files.
- **APIs and SDKs** let developers build custom content applications, connect tools, and deliver content anywhere.

An example of a website built with Sanity is Amplitude’s.

![Image of Amplitude homepage](/portfolio/prismic/060-best-headless-cms-for-developers/image-6.webp)

#### Standout features

**1\. Content Lake**

Sanity’s [Content Lake](https://www.sanity.io/content-lake) is built to give teams a real-time, structured content hub. It treats content as JSON documents, letting you store any type of content and make it instantly queryable. You can define schemas when you need them without being forced into rigid constraints, which makes evolving content models easier and importing legacy content simpler.

With GROQ (Graph‑Relational Object Queries), Sanity’s open-source query language, developers can pull exactly the content they need from any document structure. It also preserves links between content pieces, so connected data stays accurate and consistent across your project.

**2\. Sanity Functions**

[Sanity Functions](https://www.sanity.io/functions) let you automate content operations and build workflows right inside your CMS. You can package reusable functions that validate data, trigger tasks, update content automatically, and more. This means you write the logic once and use it anywhere, cutting down on repetitive coding.

Functions run directly inside Content Lake, so they have full access to your content, APIs, and schemas. You can respond to events in real time. For example, updating a document can automatically trigger translations or AI tagging.

**3\. Content Agent**

[Content Agent](https://www.sanity.io/content-agent) is Sanity’s AI built specifically for structured content. It knows your content model, follows references, and works directly with your content. The agent reads your schemas, understands relationships, and keeps everything connected.

You can generate new content, refine existing pages, or run audits across thousands of documents in minutes. Content Agent also combines your content with web research, helping you spot gaps, trends, or outdated information instantly. Also, every change is saved as a draft for review, so nothing goes live without your approval.

#### Developer benefits

Here are some key benefits developers enjoy with Sanity:

- **Single source of structured content**: The Content Lake stores all content as structured data, so you can model, query, and reuse it across any channel.
- **Easier site and app evolution**: The structured content makes it simple to update or expand your projects without starting from scratch.
- **Flexible querying**: GROQ lets you fetch and shape JSON-based content from the Content Lake.
- **Developer-friendly tools**: SDKs support popular frameworks, provide type safety, CLI tools, and libraries to help you iterate quickly and safely.
- **API options**: Sanity supports both REST and GraphQL APIs, so you can fetch and deliver content in the way that fits your project.

#### Limitations

- **Higher learning curve:** GROQ, custom schemas, and its Content Lake model can feel complex for beginners.
- **Requires developer-heavy setup:** Getting full value often depends on strong engineering involvement.

#### Pricing

Sanity offers a free plan, and its paid plans start at $15/month.

## How to choose the right headless CMS

Choosing the right headless CMS depends on how your team works, the kind of websites or apps you build, and what you value most in your workflow. There isn’t one platform that fits everyone, so it helps to look closely at a few key factors before committing to a system.

### Budget and pricing model

Some platforms charge based on usage or the number of API calls, while others use a flat monthly or annual fee. Your budget should account for more than just the subscription cost. Also, think about setup, hosting, and ongoing maintenance.

A self-hosted option may save money upfront, but often requires more developer time. Fully managed platforms handle hosting and updates for you, which reduces workload but can get expensive as your content and traffic grow.

### API type

Most headless CMSs offer REST or GraphQL APIs.

REST is widely supported, easier for beginners, and follows clear conventions. It also benefits from built-in caching at the browser or CDN level because each resource has a predictable URL. However, REST can become limiting as projects grow. Because endpoints return fixed data structures, you might end up over-fetching, increasing latency, and slowing performance.

GraphQL, on the other hand, gives developers far more flexibility. Instead of calling multiple endpoints, you can fetch all the data you need in a single query and only the exact fields you want.

### Ecosystem and integrations

A strong ecosystem can save hours of setup work. Look for CMSs that integrate smoothly and provide strong support for your preferred framework and tools such as Vercel, Netlify, or Cloudflare. Also, check for built-in extensions or plugins that integrate with the services your team uses.

### Content workflow and team experience

How your team works with content should guide your CMS choice.

If you have content-heavy teams, built-in visual editors or live previews will be very useful. They allow editors to see how a page will look before publishing, reducing back-and-forth with developers.

If your project is more developer-led, focus on CMSs that support version control, API flexibility, and local development environments. Being able to sync content models with Git or deploy through CI/CD pipelines makes development faster and easier to manage.

## FAQs

### What is a headless CMS?

A [headless CMS](https://prismic.io/blog/what-is-headless-cms) is a content management system that isn't built directly into your website, unlike [traditional platforms](https://prismic.io/blog/headless-cms-vs-traditional-cms) like WordPress or Drupal. Instead, it lives separately in the cloud, where a provider like Prismic hosts and maintains the content management interface.

Here's how it works: your content stays in the cloud-based CMS, and developers use APIs to retrieve it from the CMS whenever it's needed on your website. The CMS handles all the content storage and editing tools, while your website's frontend can be built with any technology you choose.

This separation offers two significant advantages. First, you're no longer locked into a specific technology stack—you can build with Next.js, SvelteKit, or any other framework while using the same CMS. Second, because content is delivered through APIs rather than being tightly coupled to your frontend, your site typically runs faster and more efficiently

### Why do developers prefer headless CMSs in 2026?

Developers today want the freedom to build websites with the tools and frameworks they prefer, and headless CMSs support that workflow. Instead of being tied to a CMS that controls both content and presentation, a headless setup stores content in the cloud and delivers it through an API. This removes the limitations of rigid templates or built-in templating systems, letting developers shape websites however they want.

In 2026, this approach is even more valuable. Teams are building AI-powered experiences that require flexible, structured content—whether that's feeding content to LLMs, automating translations, [generating landing pages](https://prismic.io/lp/ai-landing-page-builder), or personalizing experiences at scale. A headless CMS delivers content as structured data through APIs, making it easy to integrate with AI tools and agentic workflows. Developers can choose their preferred frameworks (such as [Next.js](https://prismic.io/blog/nextjs-seo-optimization-tips) and [SvelteKit](https://prismic.io/blog/sveltekit-vs-nextjs)), optimize for both traditional [SEO](https://prismic.io/blog/headless-cms-seo) and generative search results, and update their frontend stack over time without changing the CMS itself. This gives them a setup that remains flexible, scalable, and ready for whatever comes next—including AI-driven workflows reshaping how content is created and delivered.

### How do headless CMSs compare to traditional CMSs?

Unlike traditional CMSs, headless CMSs separate the content backend from the frontend. This affects different areas of the development experience:

- **Flexibility**: Headless CMSs let developers use any framework or technology; traditional CMSs are usually limited to built-in themes and templates.

- **Performance**: Headless systems can improve page speed and scalability, while traditional CMSs can be slower due to their monolithic architecture.

- **Collaboration**: Since they separate content and presentation, headless CMSs make it easier for developers and marketers to work together.

- **Integration**: Headless CMSs integrate easily with other tools and services; traditional CMSs may require plugins or custom workarounds.

### How do headless CMSs improve performance?

Headless CMSs improve performance in the following ways:

- They deliver content through APIs, which makes loading faster because only the required data is fetched and rendered, rather than loading full web pages.

- They allow developers to use static site generation, reducing the need for server processing since pages are pre-built and served instantly from a CDN.

- They support better caching strategies that speed up content delivery by storing responses at the edge, so repeated requests don’t have to hit the origin server every time.

- They let teams optimize each frontend for performance without being limited by a monolithic system.

- They separate the backend from the frontend, preventing heavy code from slowing page loads.

### What are the main challenges of using a headless CMS?

Common challenges include:

- Needing technical knowledge to connect APIs and handle integrations.

- Managing hosting, deployment, and security, which are often handled automatically in traditional CMSs.

- Handling costs that can rise as projects scale or require more advanced features.

- Integrating third-party tools for analytics, forms, or search that aren’t built in by default.

- Setting up content modeling correctly from the start to avoid structure issues later.

### How do APIs work in headless CMSs?

APIs in headless CMSs act as the bridge between the backend content and your frontend application. You request content from the CMS using REST or GraphQL endpoints, then display it on your website or app. This approach allows developers to use any tech stack to create websites and deliver content.
